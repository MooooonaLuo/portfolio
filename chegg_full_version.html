Context

Students are unaware of Course dashboard's value

Chegg's Course Dashboard provides various study tools to help students ace their courses in college. However, students only use Chegg to search for homework solutions and are unaware of the value of other study tools.

Project brief

Increase repeat visits to course dashboard from homepage

I didn't receive a complete project brief when we kicked off the project because the two PMs started their internships two weeks later than me. To fully understand the constraints of the project and how the project contributed to the company's business goal, I set up meetings with senior PMs in the broader team.


👥 Target Users

Students who revisit the signed-in homepage after they subscribe to Chegg (not their first session)

🎯 Scope

Design new modules on the signed-in homepage

⛳ User Need

Perceive the value of study tools available within their subscriptions

💼 Business Goal

Increase the percentage of active subscribers who revisit the Course Dashboard from 7.2% to 22%

principles

Developing three design principles

I developed three principles to create designs that meet both user and business needs. The principles ensured that my design would encourage students to explore study tools on the Course Dashboard they truly needed rather than forcing them to visit that page.


Student Centered

Incentivize students to do what they truly need

Personalized

Create personalized experience for each student

Encouraging

Avoid using demotivating or stressful information

User Flow

Map out the new user flow

We were not the only team trying to drive visits to the Course Dashboard, so I believed it was helpful to map out the new user flows within our project scope and those created by other teams. The user flows helped my team to understand how our project would contribute to the entire user journey and the higher-level business goal.


Placement

Where to place the design?

I tried different placements with consideration of information hierarchy and adjacent ongoing projects. We started with the right rail but eventually placed the module in the central area because the module had great potential to lead students to visit the Course Dashboard through a student-centered and personalized approach.

Iterations

Iterate based on feedback and user testing

I worked closely with the content design intern to generate variations on UX design and content. After discussing what to test with my UX and PM partners, the UXR intern conducted unmoderated user testing with six current subscribers.



Which idea better encourages students to study with the Course Dashboard?

We presented the two designs side by side and asked the participants how they understood each design and whether they found the design motivating.

Which variation is the most understandable?

Then we presented three variations with different chart designs and asked them what they thought of each one.

More intuitive, engaging, and actionable

After the user testing, I iterated on the design we picked based on feedback from the stakeholders, accessibility experts, visual specialists, and designers in the broader team.

How to create an encouraging and personalized weekly report?

I iterated on the Weekly Report based on user testing findings and internal feedback. Since this design was data-driven, I reached out to the engineers to understand what user data was available. Eventually, I worked with the content designer to design a dynamic CTA that encouraged students to explore underused study tools.

Final Output

Learning analysis

Happy Path

The Learning Analysis module provides a data-driven analysis of students' practice question performance. It suggests topics to focus on and drives students to study on the Course Dashboard.

Engineering Handoff

present the design rationale and discuss the backend logic

We presented our design and backend flow chart and explained the rationales to the engineering team. They were impressed by how thoughtful we were in the design of edge cases and raised one more case we had overlooked. After getting their T-shirt size, we planned to implement the Learning Analysis module first and then the Weekly report module because the latter would need more time.

Reflection

How did I tackle challenges?


Lead the team and proactively solve blockers.

Since the PM interns started the project two weeks later than the rest of us, I couldn't get input from them at the early stage. Instead of waiting for them to fully onboard, I led the team by hosting meetings, proposing more reasonable project timelines, and proactively reaching out to the adjacent project team to handle cross-team dependency.



Efficiently communicated in a team double the size of a normal team.

I worked cross-functionally with four interns, each has a manager. The interns took ownership of the project, but there were many stakeholders to be kept in the loop. Over time, I developed the skill to communicate with the right person at the right cadence. I also employed different ways to share updates on my design and collect feedback, such as video walkthroughs and meeting recaps.




Collected and synthesized feedback from 20+ people with different levels of involvement in the project.

I proactively seek feedback from the broader UX team's meetings and people in other roles. I found it tricky initially because I didn't have enough time to cover everything first, and people had different levels of knowledge about my project.

How did I efficiently collect feedback from a large group of people? I started by introducing the context and emphasizing what feedback I was looking for; due to the time limit, I focused on one or two parts that mattered the most. To handle different feedback, I kept the high-level project goal in mind and focused on feedback within the scope.
